Strong Form

Strong form equations are partial differential equations (PDEs):

  • They are met at every point in the domain
  • Must be smooth and differentiable at every point

Poisson equation

\[ \nabla^2 u = f \quad \text{in} \; \Omega = (0,1)^2 \]

With homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions (BCs)

\[ u(\partial \Omega) = 0 \quad (\partial \Omega) = \{(x, y) | x = 0, 1 \; \text{or} \; y = 0, 1 \} \]

Weak Form

Weak form equations are derived from multiplying the strong form by a test function, and integrating over the domain:

  • Do not have to be smooth or piecewise continuous, thus reducing continuity requirements

Multiplying the PDE by weight function \(v(x, y)\):

\[ \nabla^2 u = f v \]

Integrating over the domain:

\[ \int_{\Omega} v \nabla^2 u = \int_{\Omega} f v \]

[Using the rule \( \nabla (v \nabla u) = \nabla v \cdot \nabla u + v \nabla^2 u \)]

Hence:

\[ \int_{\Omega} v \nabla^2 u = \int_{\Omega} f v \] \[ \int_{\Omega} v \nabla^2 u = \int_{\Omega} (v \nabla u) - \int_{\Omega} \nabla v \cdot \nabla u \]

Using Gauss' divergence theorem:

\[ \int_{\Omega} \nabla (v \nabla u) = \int_{\partial \Omega} v \nabla u \cdot \hat{n} \, dS = 0 \]

Therefore:

\[ \int_{\Omega} v \nabla^2 u = - \int_{\Omega} \nabla v \cdot \nabla u \]

Continuity

Continuity of variables must be maintained across element boundaries The trial function and its derivatives must be continuous across element edges Continuity of displacement is C0 Strain is the 2nd derivative of displacement, so C1 continuity is needed

Enforcing Boundary Conditions

Dirichlet BCs are essential in strong form to make the solution unique:

\[ u = u_0 \; \text{on} \; \Gamma_D \]

Neumann BCs are natural, appearing naturally in weak form from integration by parts; they specify the derivative of the function at the boundary:

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = g \; \text{on} \; \Gamma_N \]

Robin BCs are a weighted combination:

\[ a u + b \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} = h \; \text{on} \; \Gamma_R \]

Comparison

Weak form is more useful for computational solving Reduces continuity requirements: lower order functions may be sufficient, reducing computational cost Easier to approximate solutions with simpler shape functions More flexible element shape/size for complex geometries
